Last Month’s Book

Her Privates We by Frederic Manning (1929)

What we liked What didn't appeal
This book probably does more than any other book to convey the comradeship, boredom, vices, unease and terror of war.
There is also a dark humour running through it - stories away from the front and regarding the work and systems the army operated at the time. The officer class is seen from a private's eyes - & it seems a balanced portrayal. The book mirrors Manning’s war & WWI seems to have left him dying slowly with eagerness only to finish this work The only book written from a private’s point of view by a classics author
Armies make war, people die and this book brings them to life.
 The elements that made the book so shocking when it was released (the swearing & accurate portrayal of officers), now seem normal & this along with the more defined class system makes the book feel dated.
He also fails to give most of the soldiers any background or introduce them properly.
The central character seems to mix with the men & the officers, but does not fit in with either. This seems an exceptional situation & perhaps compromises how accurate the observed relationships are.
The author never really opens up.
